Marilyn Milian’s Background

Marilyn Milian is of Cuban descent and was born on May 1, 1961, in Queens, New York City, USA. She gained fame as a former court judge on the long-standing TV series “The People’s Court”. Notably, she is the first Latina arbitrator on the show and continues to hold the title of longest active arbitrator in the series. Her success on TV has significantly contributed to her impressive net worth.

As of mid-2016, Marilyn Milian’s wealth was valued at $18 million due to her long-standing association with “The People’s Court”, earning an average of $5 million per year. Her continuous work on the show indicates a promising increase in her wealth.

Marilyn pursued her education at the University of Miami where she graduated summa cum laude with a degree in psychology. She later obtained her Juris Doctor from Georgetown University Law Center. Following her studies, she worked at Harvard Law School with a focus on training for the Guatemala Project.

Prior to her television career, Marilyn served as an assistant state attorney for Dade County and then went on to become a judge in the Miami Circuit Court. Her debut on “The People’s Court” began her journey as the longest running arbitrator on the show, which just completed its 30th season. Her unique style and interaction with litigants have set her apart and garnered attention from audiences.

Aside from her role on “The People’s Court”, Marilyn has appeared in several other shows such as “As the World Turns” and “The George Lopez Show”. She has earned recognition and awards for her contributions, including the “Groundbreaking Latina of the Year” award from Catalina Magazine and the National Association of Latina Leaders award.

On the personal front, Marilyn has been married to Judge John Schlesinger for over 20 years, and they have three children. She is involved in various philanthropic activities including becoming a spokesperson for the Safe Online Surfing project by the FBI, and participating in events organized by the Hispanic Community.
